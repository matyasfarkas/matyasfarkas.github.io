    MFM — MP Committee Seminar: Speaking Notes (15 min, live, no slides)¶

    Language rule: every conditional path is "the most likely projection given the conditioning assumptions" (Waggoner–Zha). Never say "entropic tilting." The consensus long-run anchors are a separate, deterministic drift.

    ⏱️ Introduction (~1 minute, spoken)¶

    "Thank you. What I'd like to show you is a tool we've built in MCM for global macro-financial forecasting and scenario analysis. The problem it solves is a practical one: when we need a coherent projection for inflation, policy rates, activity and public debt — not for one country but across the major economies and consistent with each other — we normally stitch together separate models and spreadsheets. This does it in one place, live.

    Under the hood it's a system of Bayesian VARs — around fifty economies, thirty-three of them linked into a cross-country Global VAR — with a fiscal satellite that turns four estimated fiscal variables into a debt-sustainability path, and a conditioning engine that lets you impose assumptions and read off the most likely outcome. Three things make it more than a collection of country models: it is estimated jointly on real-time, unbalanced, COVID-affected data without discarding observations; the countries are genuinely coupled, so a shock in one bloc propagates to the others; and the whole thing — central projection, scenarios, densities — exports to Excel with one click.

    So let me start with the unconditional projection, show how the variables are modeled and estimated, then build a few scenarios live and look at the international spillovers."

    1 · Unconditional / central projection — US (~3.5 min)¶

    Monetary block — what's on screen and how it's modeled - Variables in the top row: Inflation (JGDP), Policy Rate (POLRATE), GDP growth (GDPH); unemployment and yields below. - Levels vs rates (the key modeling point): "Price variables are modeled as 100·log price levels, not as inflation rates — the Minnesota prior shrinks each toward a random walk in the level, which is the right null for a near-unit-root price. The inflation you see is a display transform: four times the quarterly log-difference, annualized. The policy rate and yields, by contrast, are modeled directly as levels in percent — bounded, mean-reverting series. So it's internally consistent: a price is a level, a rate is a level, inflation is a transform of the price level." - Estimation: "Estimated with the Giannone–Lenza–Primiceri Minnesota prior — a hierarchical Bayesian VAR where the shrinkage hyperparameters are themselves estimated, so the data choose how tight the prior is, rather than us fixing it." - COVID: "2020 is handled as missing data — the macro variables in the pandemic quarters are masked and imputed inside the Gibbs sampler, conditional on the financial variables which stay observed. So a once-in-a-century outlier doesn't distort the estimated dynamics or the prior." - The anchors: "The central path is anchored to consensus long-run targets — inflation glides to ~2%, the policy rate to neutral, fiscal ratios to CBO/IMF. That's a transparent, adjustable long-run drift, and it's separate from any scenario conditioning."

    Fiscal satellite — beyond a standard macro VAR - "Government debt is not a VAR variable. The BVAR estimates four fiscal series: revenue, primary expenditure, the effective interest rate on the stock, and the stock-flow-adjustment residual. Debt is derived by the budget identity: dₜ = dₜ₋₁·(1+i)/(1+g) − pb + sfa." - The three forces: "(1+i)/(1+g) is the snowball — when the effective rate exceeds nominal growth, debt rises mechanically even with a balanced budget. pb = revenue − primary spending is the discretionary effort. The SFA is the below-the-line and valuation residual the headline deficit misses." - The result: "With the consensus anchors, US debt rises to about 143% over five years — CBO-consistent — driven by a persistent primary deficit and the effective rate converging up toward growth." - Bands: "The debt fan is produced by running this recursion per posterior draw, so the uncertainty is the model's, propagated through the identity."

    Excel hand-off - Set Uncertainty = Quick → Compute → Excel. - "One sheet per variable, organized by date: the unconditional density — 16th and 84th percentiles around the median — next to the scenario path and the deviation, plus a Summary tab with all variables side by side and a Notes tab documenting each source series and its units."

    2 · GFC corporate-yield shock — Waggoner–Zha (~4 min)¶

    • Frame the method: "Conditioning is Waggoner–Zha — you impose a path on some variables and the model returns the most likely projection for everything else, consistent with the estimated dynamics."
    • Step 1 — corporate up, 10Y free: condition Aaa corporate (FAAA) up ~150–200 bp over the next year; leave the Treasury 10-year free; Compute.
    • "Watch the risk-free 10-year — the model pulls it down. That's flight-to-quality: investors flee to safety, the risk-free curve falls, activity and inflation soften. A recession read — and it's the model reading the historical co-movement of spreads and Treasuries in a stress."
    • Step 2 — also pin the 10Y unchanged: condition 10Y (GLB_USLR), leave it flat; Compute.
    • "Now I've isolated the shock: corporate up, risk-free curve held flat — a pure credit-spread / financial-conditions tightening, with no flight-to-quality offset. That's the clean, interpretable scenario you'd hand to a stress test."
    • The takeaway: "Same corporate shock — but conditioning on the 10-year flips the whole story from a recession to a contained financial-conditions shock. Conditioning is the scenario design."

    3 · EA-yield shock → US & global spillovers — the GVAR (~3 min)¶

    • "Now the international dimension. I'll run the same yield shock, but in the Euro Area, and watch it reach the US."
    • Switch to Euro Area → condition YIELD10_EA (or EACORP) up → turn "Scenario spillovers" ON → "Solve full GVAR."
    • The mechanism: "Each country's VAR carries three trade-weighted foreign aggregates — foreign demand, foreign inflation, foreign short rate — built from its partners. The EA shock raises EA's contribution to every partner's aggregates, and the solver iterates the coupled system to a fixed point, x = Mx + d, in closed form. A single-country EA model would hold the US fixed; the GVAR lets EA tightening pull on US financial conditions, US activity, oil and global equity."
    • Switch to US: "The US block moves even though I put no US conditioning on it — that's the pure EA→US spillover. This is what lets us discuss the global transmission of a regional financial shock."
    • (Reminder: without the spillover switch / Solve full GVAR, EA moves alone.)

    4 · US oil/energy — demand vs supply (~3 min)¶

    • "Last one — how the same oil price move can mean two opposite things. Reset first, back to the US."
    • Step 1 — oil up alone: condition WTI (PZTEXP) up to ~$100, nothing else; Compute.
    • "US output goes up. Why? The model reads the historical oil–output correlation as demand-driven — historically oil rises when global demand is strong — so high oil alone pulls activity up. The demand-dominant response."
    • Step 2 — add short-term sign restrictions: keep oil up, also pin GDP growth (GDPH) down and inflation (JGDP) up at t+1..t+2 only; Compute.
    • "A couple of Cholesky-type sign restrictions on the impact quarters now identify it as an adverse supply shock: output falls, inflation rises — stagflation — with the later quarters left free for the model to fill in. The contrast is the point: conditioning is identification, and two sign restrictions turn a demand shock into a supply shock."

    Close (~30s)¶

    • "To sum up: a central projection anchored to consensus, Waggoner–Zha scenarios layered on top, genuine cross-country spillovers through the GVAR, and a one-click Excel hand-off — for fifty economies. Happy to take it anywhere you like — pick a country, pick a shock."

    🛡️ If pushed — three honest defenses¶

    • How is it estimated? (the contribution): "An EM-flavoured fixed point. The Durbin–Koopman smoother completes each country's missing and COVID-masked data given the current foreign 'star' aggregates (the E-step); each Bayesian bloc is re-estimated on the completed panel (the M-step); we iterate until the trade-weighted stars converge. So the coupled system is estimated jointly on real-time, unbalanced data — no observations dropped — which a classical fixed-weight OLS GVAR cannot do. We're explicit that it's EM in flavour, a contraction in the stars, not a convergence-guaranteed global EM."
    • Does debt feed back into rates / the deficit? "The displayed debt path is a transparent accounting recursion on the four forecast drivers plus the anchors. A Bohn fiscal-reaction function and a sovereign risk-premium are coded but dormant — a documented possible extension, not active in what you're seeing."
    • Anchors vs conditioning: "The long-run anchors are a deterministic drift to consensus targets; the Waggoner–Zha conditioning is the scenario layer on top — independent of each other, and both toggle off to reveal the raw drifting BVAR."

    Pre-flight (before the room)¶

    • Country = US, Units = "q/q ann." (not "% vs baseline" — that disables dragging).
    • Anchors confirmed ON: "Long-run targets" reads (7 set).
    • One "Reset all" for a clean start; between cases use "Reset" (keeps anchors).
    • For exports: Uncertainty = Quick → Compute first so the bands populate.
    • Financial charts live in "Global commons" (10Y GLB_USLR, Baa GLB_USBAA, oil GLB_OIL) and "Country-specific" (Aaa FAAA, WTI PZTEXP) — scroll down once to locate them.
