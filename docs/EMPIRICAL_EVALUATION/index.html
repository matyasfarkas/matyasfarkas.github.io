    Empirical Evaluation — Out-of-Sample Forecast Horse-Race & Spillover Analysis¶

    This page documents the empirical core of the accompanying paper: a rigorous out-of-sample (OOS) forecast-evaluation programme for the EM-Bayesian Global VAR, plus the endogenous-US spillover analysis the system uniquely enables. The methodology, the benchmark suite, the leakage-control invariants, the scoring metrics, and the significance machinery are all described here, together with the separate evaluation repository that implements them.

    Empirical results are IN — read them honestly

    The OOS sweep has run: 56 raw forecast origins (2010Q1–2024Q4), which leave ~55 scorable headline origins once the four 2020 origins are dropped (§4.2), × 9 models, CRPS-primary density scoring + a 90% Model Confidence Set. The headline numbers and the honest caveats are in Results and Tier-A spillover. The short version: AR(p)/DFM lead the point race, but the EM-Bayesian GVAR is inside the Model Confidence Set on the nominal block (inflation at every horizon, the short rate at h≤4), beats the textbook OLS GVAR decisively, and the endogenous-US variant weakly dominates the frozen one. The draw-based densities are over-confident (state this), and the parametric log-score column is numerically broken on the under-covered clouds — CRPS + MCS are the robust primaries.

    1. Purpose¶

    The methodology manual (User Manual PDF, §Empirical Results) lays out the estimation machinery; this page covers how that machinery is evaluated. The paper rests on two empirical pillars:

    1. An OOS forecast horse-race (Part 1). Does the EM-Bayesian GVAR forecast better than a ladder of progressively richer benchmarks — a random walk, an AR(p), a single-country BVAR, the textbook OLS GVAR, a single-pass Bayesian GVAR, and a dynamic factor model? The race is designed so two key ablations isolate (a) the value of the global coupling and (b) the value of the EM data-completion loop.

    2. An endogenous-US spillover analysis (Part 2). What does treating the US as an endogenous coupled bloc — rather than the classical frozen exogenous anchor — add? This is the channel a standard GVAR cannot even represent: a US shock that propagates abroad and feeds back into the US through the same coupled system.

    The two pillars share one principle: every score must be computed in the exact data spaces and under the exact in-sample treatments the estimator and solver use, or the comparison is meaningless. Those invariants are stated up front (§4) and enforced with explicit asserts in the harness.

    2. The separate evaluation repository¶

    To keep the production tool clean (the deployed app and its estimators never depend on evaluation code), the OOS programme lives in a separate repository:

    2.3 The protocol document¶

    The complete design + runbook lives at /home/farkas/github/em-bayesian-gvar-eval/docs/EMPIRICAL_PROTOCOL.md.     3. The horse-race design (Part 1)¶

    3.1 The proposed model — two reported variants¶

    The EM-Bayesian GVAR, exactly as pipeline/export_bvar_gvar24_loop.m runs it. Both variants of decision D9 are reported as proposed-family entries:

    • emgvar_endo — the paper's contribution: the US is an endogenous coupled bloc (US_ENDOGENOUS=1), carrying its own ROW{DEM,INF,FIN}_US stars plus a US trade-weights row, and feeding the four global commons (GLB_OIL, GLB_USLR, GLB_USBAA, GLB_USEQ) that every Rest-of-World bloc pins to.
    • emgvar_frozen — the legacy frozen-anchor GVAR: the US is estimated once as a closed anchor; the RoW cannot feed back into the US. Cheaper, and it is exactly the Part-2 spillover contrast — so having both in the race lets the horse-race itself show whether endogenizing the US helps forecasts, not only spillovers.

    Each bloc is the standard call cgl_bvarV5(x, lags=3, statCols, nDraws, nBurn, 1, H, 'mcmc',1,'MNpsi',0,'sur',0,'noc',1, ...). The EM loop is a Jacobi update: build all stars from the frozen start-of-sweep DK-completed panels, re-estimate every bloc in parfor, recompute the stars from the completed partner data, and iterate to max|Δstar| < TOL (sweep default 0.05; tighten to 0.03 for final tables) or MAXIT (default 12).

    The forecasts and densities scored are produced at solve time from the estimator's per-draw predictive object Dforecast, not from the deployed app — the app's gvarSolve.ts is for interactive scenario propagation. The GVAR coupling still enters, because the stars (hence each bloc's regressors and completed history) are jointly determined by the EM fixed point.

    3.2 The six benchmarks¶

    The suite is built so each competitor is fair and strong. Two of the entries are deliberate ablations.

    # Benchmark Id Specification (each on the same DataRaw_<CC>_glob.json, truncated ≤ t0) What it isolates
    (i) Random Walk / no-change rw (+ clim) Per target series, in the scored space: rate → last observed level carried flat; growth/inflation → the last observed value of the (already-differenced) transform carried flat (RW-in-growth, not a seasonal-RW on the level). Gaussian predictive density centred at the no-change value, scale = the in-sample SD of the h-step change estimated directly at horizon h (not √h·σ₁). A climatology (sample-mean) sibling is the second naive anchor. The universal reference; every relative-RMSE / -CRPS ratio is vs this.
    (ii) AR(p) per series arp Univariate AR on each target in its scored space, p by AIC over {1..4} (BIC and fixed p=4 as robustness). OLS with a constant; iterated (default, the strong h≥4 competitor) and direct multi-step variants. Gaussian innovation variance propagated h-steps. A strong univariate dynamic benchmark.
    (iii) Single-country Bayesian VAR sbvar The bloc estimated alone: drop the 3 ROW* star columns, keep the identical cgl_bvarV5 call (same GLP/Minnesota prior, lags=3, same 'MNpsi',0,'sur',0,'noc',1, same COVID-as-missing macro mask) and MAXIT=1. The GLP hyperprior is re-fit on the star-free panel (data-driven → fair). Value of the global coupling (vs proposed: only the RoW stars differ).
    (iv-a) Textbook OLS GVAR cgvar_a The literature GVAR (Pesaran–Schuermann–Weiner; Dees–di Mauro–Pesaran–Smith): per-bloc OLS VARX(p, q) with contemporaneous + lagged foreign stars as weakly-exogenous regressors, fixed (as-of) weights, no GLP prior, no COVID-as-missing (2020 dummied). A separate OLS-VARX path — the one piece of new estimator code in Part 1, and mandatory (without it the comparison is Bayesian-vs-Bayesian, not proposed-vs-literature). Value of the whole Bayesian + EM + COVID apparatus vs the literature GVAR.
    (iv-b) Single-pass Bayesian GVAR cgvar_b Identical to the proposed model — same cgl_bvarV5/GLP prior + COVID-as-missing, frozen-anchor US — except MAXIT=1: the stars are built once, with no EM re-convergence. Value of the EM data-completion / star re-convergence alone (vs proposed: identical except the star fixed point).
    (v) Bayesian dynamic factor model dfm A few static factors (3–5) extracted from the cross-bloc target panel (EM/PCA-with-missing on the masked panel), a Bayesian VAR(1–2) on the factors + Gaussian idiosyncratics, predictive density by simulation. A non-GVAR multivariate global benchmark — the "is the GVAR structure even needed?" competitor.

    The nesting structure that the significance tests respect (§6): (i) RW / (ii) AR ⊂ every VAR model, and (iii) sbvar ⊂ (iv-b) cgvar_b ⊂ proposed emgvar_*.

    3.3 Targets, scopes, horizons¶

    • Targets (three per bloc), resolved via the estimator's findcol order — never hard-coded indices:
    • GROWTH = real GDP growth = 4·Δ(100·log gdp level), gdp = GDP_<CC> | GDPH;
    • INFL = inflation = 4·Δ(100·log price level), price = HICP_<CC> | CPI_<CC> | JGDP;
    • RATE = short rate = the linear level (already % p.a.), rate = RATE_<CC> | POLRATE | YIELD10_<CC> (optional — CH/NO have none and drop out, and drop out of partners' ROWFIN).
    • CN GDP is real (since the 2026-06 CN-only re-estimate) → scored as 4·Δ(100·log GDP_CN) directly, the same as the other blocs (no subtraction-deflation).
    • Scopes (D3): headline = US, EA, JP, UK, CA, CN × {growth, inflation, short rate} (the body table); full = all 33 estimated blocs (rate-less blocs contribute growth + inflation), reported as median + IQR of relative-RMSE across blocs in an appendix.
    • Horizons (D7): h ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8} quarters. The estimator runs H=20; the harness slices the first eight rows of Dforecast and scores this subset.

    4. Recursive expanding window, as-of weights, and the look-ahead invariants¶

    4.1 Scheme (D1, D2)¶

    • Pseudo-real-time on the final 2025Q4 vintage (a genuine real-time vintage does not exist for most of the ~50-economy panel; a US + EA-subset real-time run is a flagged robustness extension, not the default).
    • Expanding window, recursive. First origin 2010-Q1, last 2024-Q4 (the 2010Q1–2024Q4 window spans 60 quarters; 56 raw origins are scorable once each (origin, h) is trimmed so a realization ≤ 2025Q4 exists — h=8 only for origins ≤ 2023Q4, h=4 for ≤ 2024Q4, …). The headline then drops the four 2020 forecast origins (an outlier the paper is not about), leaving ~55 scorable headline origins. COVID-as-missing still applies in-sample at every origin where 2020Q2–Q4 ≤ t0.
    • Short-sample rule. A bloc enters at origin t0 only if it has ≥ 40 in-sample quarters there (the headline six all qualify from 2010Q1).

    4.2 Rolling as-of-t0 trade weights (D11)¶

    The single biggest leakage risk is the trade weights. The shipped weights_full.json is a fixed 2021–2023 average; using it at a 2010–2020 origin injects future trade structure. Therefore, for each origin the driver rebuilds the weights from an as-of window ending ≤ t0 (rolling 3-year average, window = (Y-3)..(Y-1) for origin year Y), renormalized over the partners present, cached one file per origin-year. A fixed pre-2010 window (e.g. 2005–2009) is the cheap, leak-free fallback (it biases toward the past, never the future). The shipped 2021–2023 file is forbidden in the sweep.

    4.3 The four look-ahead invariants (asserted at every origin)¶

    oos_driver.m enforces all four with explicit asserts:

    1. Panel truncation (I1). Every DataRaw_<CC>_glob.json is sliced to dates ≤ t0 (rows + the embedded GLB_* global columns) before transformData and before any star is built. Hard assert: no surviving row may date after t0.
    2. As-of weights (I2). The chosen weights file's window must end ≤ year(t0); the deployed weights_full.json/weights_24bloc.json artefact is refused by path. (A 2024 origin's freshly-rebuilt 2021–2023 as-of file is legitimately allowed — it ends 2023 ≤ 2024 — while the shipped file is not, the only field that distinguishes them.)
    3. Star re-convergence is in-sample only (I3). buildstars reads partners' DK-completed panels; because every partner panel is itself truncated to ≤ t0, no star cell at any in-sample date can read a post-t0 realization. Asserted inside the per-origin EM loop (run_one_origin.m).
    4. No 2025Q4 harmonization at earlier origins (I4). The "2025Q4 vintage" is only the well from which ≤ t0 rows are drawn — no seasonal re-estimation, rebasing, or deflator splice that uses post-t0 data touches the ≤ t0 panel. transformData is a fixed deterministic map (100·log, 4·Δ) with no estimated parameters, so it leaks nothing.

    Determinism. cgl_bvarV5 calls rng default at entry, so a given (x, p, nDraws) is reproducible. The driver records a per-(origin, bloc) seed in each forecast JSON's meta.

    5. The per-origin forecast JSON contract¶

    For each (model, origin) the driver writes one file at results/forecasts/<model>/<origin>.json. The scorer ingests these — no MATLAB in the scoring path.

    {
  "model":  "emgvar_endo | emgvar_frozen | sbvar | cgvar_a | cgvar_b | rw | clim | arp | dfm",
  "origin": "2019-Q1",
  "blocs": {
    "US": {
      "GROWTH": { "draws": [[h1,h2,h4,h8], ...], "mean": [h1,h2,h4,h8] },
      "INFL":   { "draws": [...], "mean": [...] },
      "RATE":   { "draws": [...], "mean": [...] }
    },
    "EA": { ... }, "...": {}
  },
  "meta": { "nDraws": 200, "lags": 3, "emIters": 2, "covidInSample": false,
            "weightsWindow": "2005-2009", "usEndogenous": true,
            "horizons": [1,2,4,8], "seed": 12345 }
}

    The draws are already in the scored space (growth/inflation = 4·Δ(100·log level), rate = linear level); the scorer treats every value as a real number and does not re-transform. The columns are packed densely by requested horizon (column j holds horizon meta.horizons[j] — e.g. cols 1..4 = h = 1, 2, 4, 8), and eval/score.py reads that list from meta to map a requested h to its column. At NDRAWS=200 (nBurn = half) a typical cloud is ~100 post-burn draws × 4 horizons.

    Realizations are either embedded per cell (var_node["realized"]) or supplied separately in results/realizations.json (keyed bloc → var → target-date → value), read identically from the untruncated vintage by matlab/build_realizations.m. A cell with no realization ≤ 2025Q4 is simply not scored.

    6. Scoring and significance¶

    6.1 Point and density scores (eval/score.py)¶

    eval/score.py is pure post-processing: it never touches MATLAB or the estimator. It aligns each (model, origin, bloc, variable, horizon) forecast to the realized value and computes:

    • Point. RMSE, MAE, and the headline relative-RMSE = RMSE(model)/RMSE(RW) (<1 ⇒ beats RW).
    • CRPS — the PRIMARY density score. The unbiased empirical-ensemble estimator CRPS = (1/m)Σ|X_k − y| − (1/2m²)ΣΣ|X_k − X_l| (computed via the sorted-array O(m log m) identity), robust to non-Gaussian draw clouds and well-behaved at m≈100 draws. CRPS is the metric the conclusions lean on.
    • Log predictive score (DISCARDED — numerically broken, see §9.3). The LPS column was intended as a corroborating density score: a parametric Gaussian fit to the draw cloud, with a closed-form method-of-moments skew-normal for asymmetry and a winsorized KDE robustness check. In the realized results the parametric Gaussian log-score explodes on the under-covered GVAR-family clouds (magnitudes up to ~1e16, §9.3) — it is a scoring artifact of fitting a Gaussian to a sub-0.90-coverage cloud, not a model pathology. The LPS column is not reported; CRPS + the MCS are the density primaries.
    • Calibration. Randomized PIT of each realization in its ensemble → PIT histograms per (model, horizon), with 50% / 90% coverage and a χ² uniformity p-value.

    Density caveat — confirmed in the results (§9.4)

    Dforecast propagates only the shock + within-draw (β, Σ) MH variation forward from the last observed state; it does not integrate over the DK-smoother uncertainty of the COVID-imputed states, and the forecast is seeded from the posterior-mean completed state. The realized PIT (§9.4) bears this out: the GVAR-family 90% intervals cover only ~0.73–0.85 vs the 0.90 target, worst at h=1 — the densities are over-confident. This is why every density conclusion leans on CRPS (which is calibration-robust) and the parametric log-score is discarded (§9.3). Treat the under-coverage as a scoped, known fix (widen the bands / integrate the smoother uncertainty), not a model failure.

    6.2 Nesting-correct predictive-ability tests (eval/tests.py)¶

    The nesting structure dictates the test — the single most common referee kill-shot:

    • Giacomini–White (GW) — the HEADLINE test. Valid under nesting and recursive/expanding-scheme-correct (GW assume a finite estimation window / non-vanishing estimation uncertainty — exactly this setup), and loss-agnostic, so it is used for point and density (CRPS/LPS) differentials alike. Conditional Wald W = T·z̄'Ω̂⁻¹z̄ ~ χ²_q with instruments g_{t−1} = [1, d_{t−1}] and an optional recession / financial-stress indicator (state-dependent predictive ability).
    • Clark–West (CW) — for the nested point/MSE comparisons; the adjusted differential f_t = e_small² − [e_large² − (ŷ_small − ŷ_large)²] removes the larger model's estimation-noise penalty (one-sided: large positive ⇒ the larger model genuinely helps).
    • Diebold–Mariano (DM) — non-nested pairs only (e.g. proposed vs DFM, or vs the OLS GVAR when neither nests the other): two-sided, HAC (Newey–West) long-run variance with bandwidth ≥ h−1, plus the Harvey–Leybourne–Newbold small-sample correction (~50–60 origins is small). Standard DM is non-standard/oversized for the nested proposed-vs-benchmark pairs that are the paper's point — using it there invites rejection.

    Multiplicity (never read a single star in isolation):

    • Romano–Wolf step-down — FWER control over the family of comparisons via a moving-block bootstrap with shared resampling indices (respects cross-test dependence).
    • Model Confidence Set (Hansen–Lunde–Nason) — per (variable, horizon), the set of models statistically indistinguishable from the best (default loss = CRPS).

    The nesting map and the test selection are stated once in eval/tables.py (NESTING, is_nested) so the stars always use the correct test per pair.

    6.3 Outputs (eval/tables.py)¶

    tables.py is the last, pure-Python presentation stage. build_all() produces: the relative-RMSE matrix (headline-6 averages) + full-panel distribution; the CRPS / LPS tables with ratios vs RW; the GW / CW / DM significance stars with the nesting label, Romano–Wolf adjusted set, and per-(variable, horizon) MCS membership; and the PIT histograms + coverage with the χ² uniformity p-value. Each table is emitted as both a text grid (logs) and a nested-dict JSON blob (LaTeX/plotting).

    7. The Tier-A spillover analysis (Part 2)¶

    The shipped solver (gvarSolve.ts) computes a conditional-mean scenario response, not GIRFs/FEVDs. The protocol therefore splits Part 2 into two tiers, and decision D13 gates Tier A first:

    • Tier A — directly solver-computable (the headline, zero new estimator code). The conditional scenario propagation of a US shock, endogenous vs frozen, from solveGVARClosedFormEndo / solveGVARLinear. This is the empirical Part-2 contribution the shipped code already supports.
    • Tier B — deferred new code. The GIRF, generalized FEVD, and Diebold–Yilmaz connectedness index require a coupled-companion MA/GIRF builder. The ingredients exist in the exports (companion_mean.json carries each bloc's betahat = companion Γ(1..p) and sigmahat = Σ; the coupling M is assembled by gvarSolve.ts), so it is constructible — but it is a new module (pipeline/gvar_girf.*), validated against the Tier-A response, and is the structural extension, not the submission gate.

    7.1 The headline Tier-A contrast¶

    matlab/spillover/tier_a_scenario.m orchestrates the contrast (it shells out to eval/tier_a_scenario.mjs, since the solver is pure TypeScript and MATLAB cannot import it):

    1. Set the pin. Hold US POLRATE at baseline +100 bp over the whole horizon — a multi-period conditional projection on the entire rate path (correctly not a one-period shock or a GIRF; it answers "if the US rate were 100 bp higher throughout, what does the coupled system's conditional mean do").
    2. Solve both topologies from the same endogenous-staging exports, the frozen variant produced by stripping the US export's three ROW*_US columns in-memory so the RoW blocs and GLB_* commons are byte-identical across solves (the gap isolates exactly one channel — US endogeneity):
    3. Endogenous (US_ENDOGENOUS=true ⇒ solveGVARClosedFormEndo): the augmented state carries every bloc's ROW{DEM,INF,FIN} star deltas including the US's own (the RoW→US feedback) plus the US global-column deltas that drive every RoW bloc. ⇒ delta.US ≠ 0.
    4. Frozen (US_ENDOGENOUS=false ⇒ solveGVARLinear): the US is the exogenous driver; its scenario enters only the forcing d; there is no ROW*_US row in the fixed point. ⇒ delta.US == the pinned scenario only.
    5. Report per bloc (US, EA, JP, UK, CA, CN), in the scored space: peak (max-abs) conditional response and peak horizon for growth/inflation/rate under each topology; the gap = endogenous − frozen (the channel the frozen anchor cannot represent — the world→US direction is structurally absent, not "present but zero"); and the spectral radius ρ(M) per topology (the multiplier strength). The deployed coupling artifact coupling_default.json.gz reports rhoSpectral = 0.924 for the endogenous 33-bloc system at dim = 2060 (the frozen-anchor 24-bloc production configuration is ≈0.723 at dim = 1380). The Tier-A test itself runs on the 6-bloc US-anchored subset (US, EA, JP, UK, CA, CN) and reports, in results/tierA/us_rate_100bp.json, a frozen-route rhoSpectral ≈ 0.506 (direct inverse) versus an endogenous route solved in a single Neumann sweep (sweepCount = 1) — a different, smaller operator than the deployed 33-bloc system, so its radius is not the deployed 0.924.

    This example doubles as a solver-validation canary: the closed-form endogenous result reproduces the iterative solveGVAR fixed point to < 1e-4 across every bloc, and a warm coupling-cache hit is byte-identical (< 1e-9) to a cold rebuild — both asserted in the main repo's gvar-us-endogenous / gvar-closed-form-routing test suites (the iterative reference is itself converged to 1e-7).

    8.1 Pilot first — and the run is GATED on it¶

    The full sweep is expensive: the GVAR-EM re-runs at every origin, and the csminwel GLP-mode optimization inside each cgl_bvarV5 call is a fixed cost that does not scale with NDRAWS (so NDRAWS=200 cuts the MH/Durbin–Koopman loop ~5× but not the mode-finding). The protocol therefore gates the full run on a pilot.

    # Pilot (the gate): headline 6 blocs, ~8 origins, all models, NDRAWS=200, as-of weights.
matlab -batch "oos_driver('Origins', {'2015-Q1','2016-Q1','2017-Q1','2018-Q1', \
                                       '2019-Q1','2021-Q1','2022-Q1','2023-Q1'})"

    The pilot's three jobs: (1) validate the harness end-to-end (truncation, as-of weights, scoring, GW/CW) and the §4 invariants; (2) measure the actual per-origin wall-clock and back-substitute it into the compute budget; (3) confirm the qualitative ranking on the legible subset. Do not launch the full ~55-origin × 33-bloc run until the pilot's measured time × scale fits the budget — if it implies > 2 days serial, fan out across origins on a small cluster first.

    oos_driver.m knobs (Name/Value, each with an env fallback so a job array can set them per task): Origins, Models ({emgvar_endo, emgvar_frozen, sbvar, cgvar_b, cgvar_a, rw, arp, dfm}), Blocs (BVAR_GVAR_BLOCS, US first), NDraws (NDRAWS, default 200), Maxit (MAXIT, default 12), Tol (TOL, default 0.05), MinQ (40), WeightsMode (fixed | rolling), DropCovidOrigins (true), WarmStart, KeepScratch.

    Provenance of the numbers below

    Every figure in this section is read directly from /home/farkas/github/em-bayesian-gvar-eval/results/tables.{txt,json} (n_records_headline = 33912). The headline scope is the six anchor blocs (US, EA, JP, UK, CA, CN) × {growth, inflation, short rate} × h ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8}.     9.1 Headline read¶

    1. Point accuracy: AR(p) and the DFM lead. On relative-RMSE and point CRPS, the DFM wins every growth horizon (CRPS/RW = 0.745 / 0.836 / 0.857 / 0.841 at h = 1/2/4/8) and AR(p) wins inflation and the short rate at the short and medium horizons (e.g. INFL CRPS/RW = 0.629 at h=1; RATE CRPS/RW = 0.811 at h=1). The EM-Bayesian GVAR does not win the point race — this is stated plainly, not buried.
    2. But the EM-Bayesian GVAR is inside the 90% Model Confidence Set on the nominal block. Per (variable, horizon) MCS (Hansen–Lunde–Nason, CRPS loss, 90% level, results/tables.json["mcs"]):
      • Inflation: emgvar_endo and emgvar_frozen are in the MCS at all four horizons (h = 1, 2, 4, 8) — statistically indistinguishable from the best inflation model at every horizon.
      • Short rate: emgvar_endo is in the MCS at h = 1, 2, 4 (it drops out only at h=8, MCS p = 0.05). emgvar_frozen is already out at h=1 (p = 0.083).
      • Growth: the GVAR family is out at h=1 (emgvar_endo removed at p = 0.024) and h=8; emgvar_endo is in the MCS at h = 2 and h = 4 (emgvar_frozen too at h = 2, 4). Growth is the weak block — consistent with the point read.
    3. The endogenous-US design earns its keep (endo ⪰ frozen). In the MCS, emgvar_endo weakly dominates emgvar_frozen. The decisive case is the short rate at h=1, where emgvar_endo is in the set (p = 0.150) and emgvar_frozen is out (p = 0.083); at growth h=8 emgvar_endo is removed after emgvar_frozen (p = 0.099 vs 0.057). The two track each other closely elsewhere — the endogenous contribution is most visible in the Part-2 spillover contrast (§10), as expected.
    4. It decisively beats the textbook OLS classical GVAR. cgvar_a (literature PSW/DdMPS OLS VARX, no GLP prior, no COVID-as-missing) is out of the MCS at growth h=1/4/8, inflation h=4/8, and the short rate h=1, and posts the worst relative CRPS in the panel (up to CRPS/RW ≈ 2.5 at INFL h=1). The EM-Bayesian treatment — GLP shrinkage + COVID-as-missing + the star fixed point — is the demonstrable value-add over the literature GVAR.* This is the cleanest empirical claim the race supports.

    The single best in-family point number is emgvar_endo at inflation h=1, relative-RMSE 0.799 (and CRPS/RW 0.814) — the best of the GVAR-family entries, though AR(p) (rel-RMSE 0.641, CRPS/RW 0.629) is the overall inflation-h1 leader. The honest framing is "competitive on the nominal block, MCS-confirmed, not the point winner."

    9.3 Density: CRPS is the primary, the parametric log-score is broken¶

    CRPS/RW per cell drives every density conclusion (the MCS in §9.1 uses CRPS loss). The parametric Gaussian log-score column (LPS(G) in tables.txt) is numerically unusable for the GVAR family: on the under-covered, occasionally heavy-tailed draw clouds it explodes to absurd magnitudes (e.g. LPS(G) ≈ 1.1e15 for cgvar_b at growth h=1, ≈ 6.7e16 for emgvar_endo at growth h=8). Those are a scoring artifact of fitting a Gaussian density to a cloud whose realized PIT coverage is below target, not a model pathology. Do not report the parametric LPS; report CRPS and the MCS.

    9.4 Calibration: the densities are over-confident (state this)¶

    The randomized-PIT 90% coverage (tables.json["pit"], target 0.90) is below target for the GVAR family at the short horizon:

    emgvar_frozen 0.73 0.74 0.81 0.83

    The GVAR-family 90% intervals realize ~0.73–0.85 coverage versus the 0.90 target — they are over-confident, worst at h=1, improving with horizon. This is exactly the caveat in §6.1: Dforecast propagates only the within-draw shock + (β, Σ) variation from the posterior-mean completed state and does not integrate the DK-smoother uncertainty of the COVID-imputed states. The bands should be widened (or the smoother uncertainty integrated) before the densities are taken at face value — a scoped, known fix, not a property of the model at full coverage. CRPS, being calibration-robust, is why the conclusions lean on it.

    10. Tier-A spillover results¶

    eval/tier_a_scenario.mjs ran the US +100 bp conditional projection on the 6-bloc US-anchored subset, endogenous vs frozen, from the same endogenous-staging exports (pipeline/gvar_usendo_staging); the frozen route strips the three ROW*_US columns in-memory so the RoW blocs and GLB_* commons are byte-identical across solves. Results in results/tierA/us_rate_100bp.json.

    Read this as a multi-period conditional projection, NOT a causal IRF

    The scenario pins US POLRATE at baseline + 100 bp over the whole 20-quarter horizon — identification: "multi-period conditional projection … NOT a structural shock or GIRF" (verbatim from the JSON). The endogenous-vs-frozen gap isolates the one channel the frozen anchor structurally omits (RoW → US feedback + the global-commons amplification), and that gap is clearly non-trivial. But the magnitudes are large and sign-flipping across blocs, and a pinned multi-period projection is not a causal impulse response. The magnitudes need a validation pass (and ideally a GIRF, the deferred Tier B) before being headlined. Treat the qualitative result — "endogenizing the US opens a feedback channel the frozen anchor cannot represent" — as the claim, and the specific numbers as provisional.
