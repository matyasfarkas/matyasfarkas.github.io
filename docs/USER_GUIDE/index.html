    Monetary-Financial Model — Quick Start¶

    This tool shows you quarterly forecasts for about fifty economies — growth, inflation, interest rates, public finances, and more — and lets you bend them. Click on a chart where you think inflation will be, and every other chart updates so the whole forecast stays consistent with the estimated economics. Everything runs in your browser; nothing to install.

    This page is the five-minute walk-through. The complete manual is the User Manual (PDF): Part I, "Using the tool", covers every feature in the same style as this page — what you do, how you do it, what you find — and Part II has the full methodology. When this page and the manual disagree, trust the manual.

    Three rules that govern everything you see¶

    1. Data are data. Solid history is observed. Where a country's latest quarter has not been published yet, the model fills the gap from everything already observed elsewhere — those filled-in points are always drawn as an amber dashed tail, never disguised as data.
    2. The model is estimated once, then frozen. New data releases shift the starting point of the forecast within the year but never silently change the model. Re-estimation happens once a year, on a complete final year of data. The Monitor page (top bar) shows what each data release did to the forecasts.
    3. Judgment is visible. If you move a forecast away from what the model says — a scenario, a long-run target, a policy counterfactual — the chart says so.

    Your first scenario in five minutes¶

    1. Open the tool and pick a country. The Country dropdown in the top bar reads United States by default. The GVAR group in that dropdown lists the economies linked into the cross-country system; everything under Other countries moves on its own. Leave Units on q/q ann. and Uncertainty on None.

    2. Read one chart before touching anything. On the Inflation chart: history sits on a lightly shaded background (solid line = observed data); the forecast is the dotted line labelled Baseline, wrapped in the model's own faint grey fan. The first forecast quarter is labelled NC — short for nowcast, the model's estimate of the current, not-yet-published quarter. If a series' latest quarters are amber and dashed, the model filled them in — they are flagged, not data.

    3. Bend the policy rate. On the Policy Rate chart, click the small condition button in its header — it turns blue and reads CONDITIONED. Press the mouse down on the forecast part of the chart about four quarters in, drag half a percentage point above the dotted baseline, release. (Shortcut: right-clicking an unconditioned chart conditions it and plants the point where you clicked, in one go.) A dot marks the quarter you pinned; only that quarter is imposed — the model fills in the most plausible route through your point.

    4. Watch the rest of the economy respond. Scroll the grid: every chart has updated, not just the one you touched. With a tighter rate path, growth typically cools and inflation eases with a lag. Read it as "what usually accompanies a higher rate," not "what the rate causes" — for true cause-and-effect, use the policy counterfactual (manual Part I, §7). Other countries have not moved: cross-border spillovers are off until you switch them on (§5).

    5. Put uncertainty bands around it. In the top bar set Uncertainty to Quick and press Compute. A shaded band in the scenario's colour wraps the solid line on every chart — the middle two-thirds of plausible outcomes. It pinches shut at the quarter you dragged: you imposed that value, so there is no uncertainty about it there. Full does the same with 200 draws.

    6. Clean up. In the left control panel, click Reset at the top of the Scenario section (clears the active scenario for the current country only), and set Uncertainty back to None. You are back at the model's own view.
