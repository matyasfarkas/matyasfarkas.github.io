    Euro-area cross-border spillover & harmonization¶

    This page explains how the tool links the euro-area aggregate (EA) and its individual members (DE, FR, IT, ES, …) so that a scenario built on one bloc reaches the others in an economically consistent way — and how the shared building blocks of a monetary union (the ECB policy rate, the risk-free curve, sovereign spreads) are kept coherent across all of them.

    If you just want the "how do I use it" version, jump to Using it, step by step.

    The setup: why members need special treatment¶

    Each member (DE, FR, IT, …) is estimated as its own standalone BVAR. The EA aggregate is a full bloc of the coupled GVAR (it trades and co-moves with the US, China, etc.). The members are not in the GVAR coupling — they hang off the EA.

    That design is deliberate (a member's domestic detail — its fiscal block, its house prices, its sovereign spread — lives in the member model, while cross-border transmission is carried by the aggregate). But it creates three gaps that this feature set closes:

    1. A US or EA scenario you build would never reach a member, because the member isn't in the coupled solve.
    2. The ECB policy rate — one common instrument — was being forecast separately by each member model, so it came out different across members.
    3. There was no direct handle on the euro-area risk-free curve or a country's sovereign spread in the member view.

    1. Cross-border spillover: a US/EA scenario reaches the members¶

    When you condition the US or the EA aggregate and press ▶ Solve full GVAR, the tool solves the complete coupled system (including the EA bloc's response). It then reads the EA aggregate's solved deviation and maps it onto each member's foreign/rest-of-area inputs, so the member's own model transmits the area-wide shock into its domestic GDP, inflation, and rates.

    • The member you are viewing shows a small amber note — "Cross-border spillover from the US/EA scenario not yet computed" — with a ▶ Solve full GVAR button, until you run the solve. This is deliberate: the full coupled solve takes a few seconds, so it runs only when you ask for it (pressing the button, or Compute), never automatically on every click.
    • The transmission uses the deviation of the EA path from the member's own baseline (not the raw EA level), so it never injects the gap between the EA and the member's baseline — at a zero scenario it is an exact no-op.

    2. The ECB policy rate is common across EA and members¶

    The problem. The euro area has one policy rate, set by the ECB. But because every member was a separate BVAR forecasting its own POLRATE column, the common rate came out different across members — and sometimes implausibly so:

    bloc old terminal ECB-rate forecast
    EA 2.25%
    DE 0.71%
    FR 2.55%
    IT 3.02%

    (This was not the long-run "consensus drift" target — the policy rate has no long-run anchor in any bloc. It was purely the separate-model artefact.)

    The fix. Every member's policy rate is now pinned to the EA aggregate's POLRATE_EA path — the single common ECB rate — in both the baseline and every scenario. Concretely, POLRATE_<member> is treated as exogenous and driven by the EA rate; the member's own model then re-solves its domestic block in response to the common rate. So:

    • The policy-rate chart is identical across EA and all members.
    • Each member's domestic GDP/inflation baseline responds to the common rate — e.g. Germany, whose rate rises from the old 0.7% to the ~2.25% area path, now shows the correspondingly tighter activity; Italy (rate falling 3.0→2.25%) the opposite.
    • Scenario deviations are unchanged where you don't touch the rate: because the same common-rate pin sits under both the baseline and the scenario, it cancels in (scenario − baseline). Only the level baseline shifts.

    One consequence worth knowing

    Because the ECB rate is now exogenous and common, conditioning a member variable that would otherwise induce an endogenous domestic rate response (e.g. a large GDP shock) leaves the policy rate on the common path rather than letting the member's own rate drift. That is the correct behaviour for a single area-wide policy rate — the ECB does not set a Germany-specific rate.

    3. Conditioning the ECB rate on a member = an area-wide shock¶

    The ECB Policy Rate now appears as a draggable chart in the member's first row. Because it is the common rate, conditioning it on any euro bloc is an area-wide monetary shock:

    • Drag the ECB-rate path on, say, Germany, then press ▶ Solve full GVAR.
    • Your pin is mirrored onto the EA aggregate's POLRATE_EA, the coupled system re-solves, and the new rate propagates to the EA aggregate and every other member.
    • It is one shared rate — editing it on DE then FR edits the same underlying ECB path (last edit wins).

    4. The euro risk-free curve (Bund) and the country spread¶

    Members carry their long rate as Bund + spread: a common risk-free benchmark (the German 10-year, YIELD10_DE) plus a country-specific sovereign spread (SPREAD_<member>). Both are now surfaced as draggable charts:

    • EA Long Yield (Bund). Conditioning the benchmark is a common risk-free curve shock: it maps one-for-one onto the area long yield and propagates to every member's risk-free reference.
    • Sovereign Spread (vs Bund). Conditioning a member's spread is a country-specific stress, and it is contagious: it feeds the euro-area long yield in proportion to that country's share of euro-area GDP. Formally, Δ YIELD10_EA = w_member · Δ spread_member, which is exactly the mechanical identity for a GDP-weighted area yield (YIELD10_EA ≈ Bund + Σᵢ wᵢ · spreadᵢ). So a widening Italian spread (weight ≈ 14%) transmits far more to the area than an Estonian one (≈ 0.3%).

    The GDP weights are real Eurostat data (current-price GDP, 4-quarter-trailing share of the EA20, app/public/data/bvar/gvar/ea_gdp_weights.json) — not assumed. If both a Bund pin and a spread pin are set, their effects add on the area yield.

    Using it, step by step¶

    1. Build the scenario where it's natural.
      • Area-wide monetary or curve shock → condition ECB Policy Rate or EA Long Yield (Bund) on any euro bloc.
      • Country-specific sovereign stress → condition Sovereign Spread (vs Bund) on that member.
      • A global/US shock → condition the US or EA aggregate.
    2. Press ▶ Solve full GVAR (or Compute). The coupled system solves and the shock propagates to the EA aggregate and every member. On a member, the amber prompt tells you when a solve is pending.
    3. Switch blocs to read the transmission. View the EA aggregate and the other members to see the propagated paths; the policy-rate chart will read the same everywhere.

    Honest caveats¶

    • The common rate is identical to within a sub-basis-point (~0.005 pp) numerical residual — the in-browser smoother differs infinitesimally from the stored baseline. It is invisible on the charts; a two-decimal tooltip can very occasionally show e.g. 2.24 vs 2.25.
    • Spread contagion is a modelling choice, not an estimated equation: a country's spread feeds the area yield by its GDP weight. It is a transparent, data-grounded pass-through, deliberately simple.
    • Germany is the risk-free anchor: its YIELD10_DE is the Bund, so DE carries a Bund chart but no sovereign-spread chart (its spread is zero by construction).
    • Everything here is forecast conditioning (the Durbin–Koopman smoother), layered on top of the frozen estimated models — no re-estimation is involved.
