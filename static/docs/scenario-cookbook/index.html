    Scenario Cookbook¶

    The Scenario Cookbook is an instant-load library of named, documented conditioning recipes, reachable from the Cookbook ▾ button in the Control Panel's scenario header (next to Save / Load). It replaces the old download-only Example link: instead of downloading a spreadsheet and re-importing it, you pick a recipe and load its conditioning into the active scenario with one click.

    Each recipe is authored for the three fully-instrumented blocs — US, EA, CN — with bloc-specific variable IDs and restriction counts, and embodies a deliberate two-stage pedagogy:

    • Load naive — condition the head variable(s) alone and let the model fill in everything else. The result is the model's own read of that single move, which is usually demand-led (the BVAR's estimated business-cycle co-movement dominates). This is frequently the wrong economic story — the point is to expose it.
    • Load + restriction — add a small number of short-run pins that restrict the joint response into the economically-meaningful shock (e.g. force output down while prices rise → a supply shock). The contrast between the two stages is the entire lesson: same head move, qualitatively different — often sign-flipped — outcome once the identification is imposed.

    Every recipe is grounded in a real, citable source. Magnitudes and signs are calibrated to a published IMF/FSAP adverse stress scenario or a well-documented historical episode (cited in each section). The numbers are economically defensible, not illustrative placeholders — but they remain authored (a designer's reading of the cited path into this model's deviation space), surfaced here for the user (an IMF economist) to review and retune. The structure (which variables, naive vs restricted) is the fixed design.

    How it works (mechanics)¶

    • Recipes live in app/public/data/cookbook/cookbook.json, fetched once at load (like default_anchors.json). Each recipe is keyed by spec variable ID — not column index, because indices differ across blocs — and resolved against the active bloc's spec.json at click time (app/src/lib/data/cookbook.ts, resolveVarId → resolveRecipeStage).
    • A pin's values are stored as-is in the store's deviation space, exactly the convention the conditioning sliders and the Excel-import path use:
    • kind: "log" — a q/q-annualized growth deviation (e.g. GDP −3 = −3 % q/q annualized off baseline for that quarter).
    • kind: "lin" — a level deviation (yields/rates in pp; fiscal ratios in pp of GDP; 0 with active: true = pinned unchanged).
    • kind: "levelDisplay" — a direct 100·log level deviation for level-displayed globals (GLB_OIL, GLB_USEQ); e.g. oil +58.8 ≈ +80 % in price, equity −22.3 ≈ −20 %.
    • Loading a stage calls updateScenario, which REPLACES (not merges) the conditioning fields, so any prior pins are wiped — identical to the Excel-import behaviour. A variable referenced by a recipe but absent from the current bloc is skipped and surfaced in a small transient note (it is never silently dropped).
    • GLB_OIL is a global-block variable: conditioning it propagates to every bloc automatically at solve time (gvarSolve.ts applyGlobalPins), so the energy recipe is global by construction.
    • Cross-country spillovers (v1 scope). A recipe writes only the active bloc's pins. To see the cross-country answer, press Solve full GVAR after loading (the existing ChartGrid mirror effect propagates the active pins into gvarPins). Multi-bloc-in-one-click recipes are a deferred follow-up.

    1 · Credit crunch / spread shock¶

    Source. Federal Reserve, 2020 Severely Adverse supervisory scenario (Feb 2020) — the macro-financial backbone of US bank stress testing and of the IMF United States FSAP 2020 (Country Report 20/242), which documents a wave of corporate-sector defaults amplified by record NFC debt/GDP.

    Transmission. A corporate-credit-supply contraction. Default risk on highly-levered firms is repriced, so the IG corporate-to-Treasury spread blows out (to 5.5 pp by 2020Q3, +400 bp vs 2019Q4), and external finance becomes scarce and dear. Simultaneously a flight to safety bids up Treasuries so the risk-FREE 10y FALLS (to 0.75 % on impact) and the policy rate is cut to ~zero — the entire move is in the spread (risk premium), not the safe curve. The higher cost of risk-bearing chokes investment and hiring; output collapses (−8.5 % peak-to-trough), unemployment rises to 10 %, and the shortfall is disinflationary (CPI to ~1.25 %). Equity falls −50 % (VIX 70); house/CRE −28 %/−35 %; the USD appreciates ex-JPY and EA/UK/JP enter severe recessions.

    • Naive — corporate yield up to the documented +400 bp peak (FAAA, front-loaded to 2020Q1–Q3, fading ~2 yr), nothing else pinned. The model's estimated comovement drags the whole curve up together — a generic rate tightening — and, because business-cycle comovement is demand-led, can even read it with the wrong, mildly inflationary sign.
    • Restricted — same +400 bp spread, plus the documented credit-crunch co-movement: the safe 10y DOWN (GLB_USLR flight-to-safety, to ~0.75 % on impact — not flat), output down (GDPH, ~−8.5 % peak-to-trough), inflation down (JGDP, disinflation), equity −50 % (GLB_USEQ), and the policy rate cut to the floor (POLRATE). Spread WIDE while the safe rate FALLS is the credit-SUPPLY identification — the cost of risk-bearing rises as the safe curve drops, not a level shift in rates.

    Verified on the live US engine: naive drags the 10y up (+2.45 pp avg q0–7) with the corp shock; the restriction pushes the safe 10y down (−0.56 pp, flight-to-safety) while the spread stays wide (+2.56 pp) and the output hit deepens sharply (−7.3 % vs +0.3 %).

    Country notes. US: corp = FAAA, safe 10y = GLB_USLR, equity = GLB_USEQ, policy = POLRATE. EA: corp = EACORP, safe 10y = YIELD10_EA, policy = POLRATE_EA (EA enters a severe recession in the scenario). CN has no separate corporate yield — CORP_CN is the sovereign 10y, so a "pure IG spread" is not identifiable; the CN override widens CORP_CN to a smaller +250 bp (managed, lower beta) and holds the 3M interbank rate (RATE_CN) unchanged, isolating a term/credit-premium move at the long end with the short rate fixed, plus the shared global equity decline.

    What changed vs the prior recipe. The spread was rescaled +150 bp → +400 bp (the documented adverse move), and the restricted safe-rate leg was corrected from "hold 10y flat" to "safe 10y DOWN on flight-to-safety" — the prior flat pin contradicted the defining feature of a documented credit crunch (the safe long rate FALLS). Output-down / disinflation / equity-down / policy-cut legs were added so the supply identification is unambiguous.

    2 · Energy supply shock (global)¶

    Source. IMF World Economic Outlook, April 2026 ("Global Economy in the Shadow of War", Ch.1), ADVERSE Middle-East energy-supply scenario; transmission elasticity from ECB Working Paper No. 2905 (2024); historical anchor the 1973–74 OPEC embargo (Federal Reserve History).

    Transmission. An adverse-supply (cost-push) shock. A crude/gas-supply disruption raises the energy import bill → first-round pass-through to headline inflation (ECB WP 2905: ~+0.6 pp HICP per +10 % gas supply shock at 1 yr) and a terms-of-trade / real-income squeeze for energy importers that compresses real demand → output down while prices are up (stagflation; cf. 1973–74: oil ~4×, US CPI 12.3 % in 1974, real GDP −2.1 %). The WEO adverse scenario adds a financial-conditions leg: corporate risk premia +50 bp (advanced)/+100 bp (EM ex-China), EM sovereign spreads +50 bp, equity down, inflation-expectations drift — net global growth cut 3.1 % → 2.5 %, inflation 4.4 % → 5.4 %. The BVAR's unconditional oil comovement is dominated by demand-led oil episodes, so conditioning oil alone gives the wrong-signed "demand read" (output up).

    • Naive — oil up +80 % (GLB_OIL level-display +58.8 = 100·log 1.80, ~1 yr hump then unwinding), domestic block free. The demand-led comovement can show output rising — clearly wrong for a supply disruption.
    • Restricted — keep the oil path and add the supply + financial-conditions co-movement: real GDP down (US ~−3.5, EA ~−4, CN ~−2.5 q/q-ann, decaying over ~2 yr), inflation up (anchored to ECB WP 2905, scaled to gas +160 %), corporate spread +50 bp (FAAA/EACORP; +100 bp for CORP_CN), and equity ~−12 % (GLB_USEQ). This converts a textbook goods-market oil shock into the full macro-FINANCIAL adverse scenario.

    Verified on the live US engine: naive (oil-only) output +0.4 % q0–3 (demand read); restricted output down (< −0.3 %) with inflation higher — the restriction flips the output sign.

    Country notes. gdp = GDPH/GDP_EA/GDP_CN; infl = JGDP/HICP_EA/CPI_CN; corp = FAAA/EACORP/CORP_CN; equity = GLB_USEQ. EA is the most oil-import-intensive bloc, so its output/inflation hit is deepest; CN's GDP_CN is real and its managed/subsidised pass-through warrants a cushioned calibration. A severe variant maps to the WEO severe leg: oil +100 % sustained (GLB_OIL +69.3), premia +100 bp (AE+China)/+200 bp (EM ex-China), growth cut ~−1.1 pp.

    What changed. Oil rescaled +40 % → +80 % (the WEO adverse path); the GDP/inflation horizon lengthened from 2 q to the ~2-year energy hump; and the financial leg (corporate spreads + equity) was added — the WEO scenario is explicitly macro-financial, which the prior pure-goods-market recipe omitted.

    3 · Fiscal loosening → twin deficits¶

    Source. IMF 2018 Article IV Consultation (Country Report No. 18/207, July 2018) and the 2018 External Sector Report, assessing the 2018 US Tax Cuts and Jobs Act + Bipartisan Budget Act.

    Transmission. A procyclical fiscal expansion onto an economy already at full employment. The loosening widened the GG structural primary deficit by ~1 pp of potential GDP (−2.7 % → −3.8 %, 2017→2019) and lifted GG gross debt ~106 % → ~113 % of GDP. With demand outrunning supply, the Fed must "raise policy rates at a faster pace", which together with the demand pull puts upward pressure on the dollar (judged 8–16 % overvalued), so imports rise and the current-account deficit widens to ~3½ % of GDP by 2019–20, the NIIP falling ~8 pp / 5 yr — the saving-investment identity CA = Sprivate + Sgov − I made concrete (twin deficits). Near-term growth boost ~+0.9 pp (real GDP 2.3 % → 2.9 %), deflator 1.8 % → 2.8 %.

    • Naive — primary expenditure up ~+1 pp of GDP (FPEXP_GDP) and revenue down ~−0.5 pp (FREV_GDP), a ~1.5 pp primary loosening matched to the episode, fading after ~2 yr. The model reads a generic demand stimulus: output/inflation rise and the fiscal block turns debt/GDP up — but it does not, on its own, reproduce the external leg.
    • Restricted — keep the fiscal pins and add the IMF twin-deficits identification: a +0.9 q/q-ann GDP impulse for ~1 yr (the documented above-potential boost) and the procyclical-at-full-employment restriction — the policy rate up ~+50 bp (POLRATE, the Fed "raises at a faster pace" → upward dollar pressure → wider CA). The pedagogical payload is the joint deterioration: debt/GDP up (fiscal block) and, once the external/NFA satellite is live, trade balance / net-foreign-assets down.

    Verified on the live US engine: the fiscal drivers are conditioned in both stages (the debt read-through is the payload); the explicit demand + policy-rate restriction lifts output vs the naive fiscal-ratio read (restricted > naive by a material margin).

    Country notes. FREV_GDP / FPEXP_GDP, GDP (GDPH/GDP_EA/GDP_CN) and the policy rate (POLRATE/POLRATE_EA/POLRATE_CN) exist in all three blocs. CN uses smaller fiscal, demand and policy-rate moves given its automatic stabilisers and managed external account (ESR CA norm −¼ % for China vs −¾ % for the US), so its twin-deficits read-through is deliberately weaker.

    What changed. Fiscal magnitude rescaled from a ~3 pp primary loosening (+2 / −1) to the documented ~1.5 pp (+1 / −0.5); the demand impulse softened from +2 (2 q) to +0.9 (~4 q); and a policy-rate-up restriction was added — the missing monetary leg that actually identifies the twin-deficit channel (stronger dollar → wider CA), rather than relying on an outsized demand impulse alone.

    4 · Monetary behind-the-curve / risk-off¶

    Source. Federal Reserve 2020 Severely Adverse scenario (released 6 Feb 2020) — the published macro-financial path underlying the IMF FSAP United States 2020 bank solvency stress test (Country Report No. 20/247, Aug 2020).

    Transmission. An abrupt collapse in risk appetite reprices the equity and credit risk premia simultaneously: equities crash −50 % (VIX 70), the IG corporate spread widens to 5.5 pp (+4.0 pp), and a flight to safety pushes the safe 10y DOWN (to 0.75 %). The widening external-finance premium chokes investment/consumption → a deep, disinflationary recession (GDP −8.5 %, unemployment 10 %). The identifying restriction for a behind-the-curve variant is to pin the policy rate flat against this tightening — forcing the central bank to under-respond, which makes the recession deeper than the endogenous reaction function would deliver.

    • Naive — condition only the headline lever, the corporate spread up ~+200 bp (FAAA, scaled below the full Fed +400 bp crisis), with policy and everything else free. The model returns its generic "financial-conditions tightening" read: output/inflation soften and the policy rate eases endogenously. Inflation is left free and falls — the shock is disinflationary.
    • Restricted — impose the full identified risk-off: equity ~−25 % (GLB_USEQ), corporate spread up, the safe 10y DOWN (GLB_USLR, flight-to-safety), and the policy rate pinned flat (POLRATE = 0, active) — the bank under-reacts ("behind the curve"), so the tightening transmits fully to demand.

    Country notes. equity = GLB_USEQ (global); corp spread = FAAA (US) / EACORP (EA); safe 10y = GLB_USLR (US) / YIELD10_EA (EA); policy rate = POLRATE / POLRATE_EA / POLRATE_CN. CN has no traded IG corporate spread (CORP_CN is the sovereign 10y), so the CN block uses CORP_CN with a POSITIVE sign — in a global risk-off EM sovereign yields rise via capital outflow and a sovereign-risk-premium channel, the opposite of the AE safe-haven bid — and takes equity from the global GLB_USEQ. Magnitudes are ~half the full Fed crisis severity (a sharp repricing, not a once-in-a-generation crash).

    What changed. The recipe was previously internally incoherent — it pinned inflation up (a behind-the-curve inflationary story) while imposing a flight-to-safety disinflationary risk-off block. The inflation-up pin is removed (inflation now free, and falls); naive is reframed as the bare headline (corp spread up); magnitudes rescaled toward the cited source (equity −20 % → −25 %, safe 10y −50 bp → ~−100 bp); and the CN safe-haven leg sign-flipped to a positive EM sovereign-yield move.

    5 · AI productivity boost¶

    Source. IMF Working Paper WP/25/76, "The Global Impact of AI: Mind the Gap" (Cerutti et al., April 2025) — GIMF-GVC simulations of an AI-driven TFP shock; historical analogue the 1995–2004 US ICT boom (McKinsey 2001; FRBSF Economic Letter 2015-04).

    Transmission. A positive supply (TFP) shock. AI diffusion raises total factor productivity, lifting potential output and capitalising higher future earnings into equity. The price side is two-phase (the heart of the identification): (i) short run — investment/wealth effects lift demand before capacity installs, so inflation ticks up modestly and central banks tighten modestly; (ii) medium run — productivity expands supply faster than demand and inflation declines (output up while inflation falls = the supply signature). The paper's central result is a gap: 10-yr output gain ~+5.4–5.6 % (US) and ~+4 % (global) high-TFP, with EM gains ~+3.0–3.5 % — advanced economies gain >2× low-income.

    • Naive — real GDP growth up by a realistic ~+1 pp/yr (US +1.0, EA +0.8, CN +0.6 q/q-ann for 2 yr), prices free. The model's demand-led comovement shows inflation rising with output — the wrong sign for a supply expansion.
    • Restricted — keep the output pins and add (i) an inflation pin with the documented two-phase shape — a small short-run overshoot that turns to disinflation by ~q4 (JGDP from +0.4 to −0.5) — and (ii) an equity pin up ~+14 % (GLB_USEQ). Output up while inflation ends down flips identification from a demand boom to a positive supply shock.

    Verified on the live US engine: restricted output up (> +1 % q2–7), medium-horizon inflation negative and below the naive read, equity up — a clean positive supply shock.

    Country notes. gdp = GDPH/GDP_EA/GDP_CN; infl = JGDP/HICP_EA/CPI_CN; equity = GLB_USEQ. Magnitudes are asymmetric (US > EA ≳ CN), reflecting the paper's "gap" result. CN's GDP_CN is real, so "output up, CPI contained" reads directly as a real supply shock.

    What changed. Output rescaled +3 → ~+1 pp/yr (the old +3 for 8 q compounded to ~+6 % in 2 yr, ~5–6× the IMF decade path); the inflation restriction reshaped from flat-down to up-then-down (capturing the documented short-run demand overshoot); and magnitudes made asymmetric across blocs instead of identical.

    6 · Structural supply-chain collapse¶

    Source. Liu & Nguyen, "Global Supply Chain Pressures and U.S. Inflation", FRBSF Economic Letter 2023-14 (June 2023); output side from Kemp, Portillo & Santoro, IMF Working Paper No. 2023/042 (Feb 2023) and the IMF WEO Update (Jan 2022). The episode: the 2021–22 pandemic supply-chain bottleneck.

    Transmission. A goods-demand surge meets constrained supply — port congestion, intermediate-input/ semiconductor shortages, upstream Asian production stoppages (2021–22 China port closures, 2022 Shanghai lockdown). Scarce inputs propagate downstream, lifting input/import prices (Liu-Nguyen: a 1-SD GSCPI shock raises US headline PCE ~+0.5 pp, goods inflation up to ~+1.5 pp; GSCPI shocks added ~+2.5 pp to headline inflation at the 2021–22 peak, ~60 % of the above-trend run-up) while curtailing output (Kemp-Portillo-Santoro: 2021 disruptions subtracted 0.5–1.2 % from global value added; WEO Jan-2022 growth markdowns −1.2 pp US, −0.8 pp China, −0.4 pp euro area). Net stagflationary — output down, prices up. Crucially the effect is ~1 year with decay (Liu-Nguyen: "statistically vanishing about a year after the impact"), not multi-year.

    • Naive — condition only the trade lever (US/EA imports down ~−3 q/q-ann for 3 q; China, the upstream source, exports down ~−6). The model treats weak trade as a demand signal and tends to pull inflation down — the wrong sign for a bottleneck.
    • Restricted — add the supply identification, scaled to the evidence: output down by the documented (modest) amounts — US GDP ~−1.2 pp, EA ~−0.4/−0.5 pp, CN ~−0.8 pp — with inflation up and goods-led (US/EA ~+1.5 to +2.5 q/q-ann peak), all decaying inside ~4–5 quarters.

    Country notes. imports = MH (US) / IMP_EA; gdp = GDPH/GDP_EA/GDP_CN; infl = JGDP/HICP_EA. China sits upstream, so its export/production stoppage (EXP_CN) is the front-line channel and a source of others' import-price spikes — but China itself ran near-zero/disinflationary CPI in 2021–22 (it exported disinflation), so the CN block holds output down with prices flat (no domestic inflation-up restriction), sharpening the "China exports disinflation while bottlenecking supply" lesson.

    What changed. Magnitudes cut sharply to the documented modest, ~1-year episode: GDP −4 → −1.2, imports −8 → −3, horizon 6+ q → ~4 q. The naive import-volume drop was trimmed (in 2021 US goods imports rose — the problem was prices/congestion, not collapsing volumes), and the CN inflation-up pin was removed (China was disinflationary, not a domestic-inflation recipient).

    7 · Deglobalization / trade compression¶

    Source. IMF Staff Discussion Note SDN/2023/001, "Geoeconomic Fragmentation and the Future of Multilateralism" (Aiyar et al., Jan 2023); FDI channel from IMF WEO Apr 2023, Ch.4; price pass-through from Amiti, Redding & Weinstein (JEP 2019) on the 2018-19 US-China trade war.

    Transmission. A structural retreat from trade integration. Higher cross-bloc barriers curtail both export demand and import supply (trade volumes compress permanently); the SDN estimates trade fragmentation alone lowers global GDP 0.2 % → ~7 % (baseline ~1.2 %; technological decoupling raises EM losses to 8–12 %), with cross-bloc FDI curtailment adding ~2 % (WEO Ch.4). Re-shoring raises the price LEVEL of tradables (Amiti-Redding-Weinstein: complete pass-through of 2018 tariffs into US prices — a one-off level step), and commodity-market segmentation is inflationary specifically for the import-dependent advanced bloc while the surplus exporter faces a demand/output loss that is closer to disinflationary — so the inflation effect is asymmetric, not uniform.

    • Naive — exports and imports down, front-loaded then easing (cumulative ~−9 % volume; the CN export leg deeper at ~−14 %, since the surplus exporter is hit hardest), prices free. The model reads a symmetric trade contraction largely as a wash for activity and leaves inflation little changed.
    • Restricted — keep the trade compression and add a modest, front-loaded-then-fading price-LEVEL step in the import-dependent advanced blocs only (US JGDP / EA HICP_EA, ~+1.3–1.5 % cumulative over six quarters then reverting) — a one-off pass-through step, NOT a permanent +1 pp inflation rate. China is not pinned up on inflation: as the surplus exporter facing a demand/output loss, its domestic price effect is ambiguous-to-disinflationary, so the CN block applies a small negative deviation.

    Country notes. exports = XH/EXP_EA/EXP_CN; imports = MH/IMP_EA/IMP_CN; infl = JGDP/HICP_EA/CPI_CN. The CN override deepens the export compression (surplus exporter hit hardest) and removes/negates the inflation pin. The recipe also reads through to the external block.

    What changed. The inflation pin changed from a permanent +1 pp inflation RATE applied uniformly to all blocs (which compounds the price level ~+8 % over 8 q and mis-signs China) to a front-loaded one-off price-LEVEL step in the advanced blocs only, with China disinflationary — matching the documented complete-pass-through level effect and the SDN's bloc-asymmetric inflation finding. Trade magnitudes are anchored to the SDN severe two-bloc tail.

    8 · Inflation de-anchoring¶

    Source. EBA/ECB 2023 EU-wide banking-sector stress-test ADVERSE scenario (ECB-PUBLIC, Jan 2023) — a documented wage-price-spiral / de-anchoring path; cross-checked against the IMF GFSR Oct 2023 global stress test and the China 2024 FSSA stagflation case.

    Transmission. A cost-push energy/commodity supply shock (oil +54 %, gas to 180 EUR/MWh; 53 % of the HICP impact) collides with a wage-price spiral: real-income losses drive higher wage claims, firms pass costs into prices (second-round effects ~60 % of the HICP-level rise), expectations and wage claims ratchet up, and EA HICP stays 9.7 % (2023) → 5.3 % (2024) → 3.8 % (2025) — persistently above target even as growth turns negative (stagflation). "The increase in current and expected inflation … leads to higher market interest rates": de-anchored expectations are priced into the long end as a higher term/inflation-risk premium — EA 10y 2.79 % → 5.42 % → 4.54 %; US 10y 3.62 % → 5.82 % → 4.87 % (curves invert). It is a cost-push shock that de-anchors expectations, not a demand shock — which is why inflation does not mean-revert and the term premium rises.

    • Naive — inflation up +4 q/q-ann in the first year only, then free (JGDP/HICP_EA/CPI_CN). The model's mean-reversion pulls inflation back toward its anchor — a transitory spike that fades. The "well-anchored" baseline.
    • Restricted — hold inflation +2 q/q-ann through the long horizon (matching the ~+1.8 pp yr-3 residual above target) and impose the term-premium repricing as an abrupt, front-loaded jump (not a slow build): EA 10y +260 bp in yr 1 easing to +175 bp (YIELD10_EA); US 10y +220 bp easing to +125 bp (GLB_USLR); CN sovereign a muted +100 bp → +75 bp (CORP_CN, the China FSSA imported-oil-supply case with a smaller, partly de-coupled domestic rate response).

    Verified on the live US engine: naive long-horizon inflation fades (transitory); restricted persists (> +1.5 % q10–15) and lifts the long 10y yield vs naive (the term premium).

    Country notes. infl = JGDP/HICP_EA/CPI_CN; long yield = GLB_USLR (US) / YIELD10_EA (EA) / CORP_CN (CN sovereign-10y proxy). Magnitudes are bloc-specific (calibrated to the EBA adverse 10y paths and the IMF China FSSA), not held identical.

    What changed. The naive spike was raised +3 → +4 (the EBA peak was 9.7 %), and the long-yield pin was raised from a gradual +100 bp to an abrupt, front-loaded +220–260 bp — the documented de-anchoring repricing is a sudden term-premium jump, not a slow build, and the prior pin understated the peak ~2× and mistimed it.

    9 · Fiscal default likelihood ↑ (sovereign risk premium)¶

    Source. IMF GFSR October 2025 ("Shifting Ground beneath the Calm", Ch.1) + Fiscal Monitor April 2026 on the erosion of the US Treasury safety/convenience premium (deficit 7–8 % of GDP, gross debt → ~142 % by 2031 / ~156 % by 2055); the distress co-movement calibrated from the Fed 2024 Severely Adverse scenario; the sovereign-spike-with-distress + sovereign-bank loop from the euro-area sovereign crisis 2010-12 (Italy 10y 4 % → 7 %, Spain > 7 %) and the IMF Spain 2024 FSSA (cumulative −7 pp real GDP).

    Transmission. As perceived sovereign-default risk rises, the bond loses its safe-haven status, so the 10y govt yield rises via a higher term/risk premium EVEN AS activity weakens — it does not fall in the usual flight-to-quality way. This is the identifying signature: in the Fed's 2024 severely-adverse generic recession the 10y falls 3.7 pp, the opposite of the fiscal-risk leg. The sovereign risk premium passes through to private credit (corporate, esp. lower-rated Baa/BBB, spreads widen; banks holding sovereign debt take losses and tighten — the sovereign-bank loop), and higher debt-service feeds the self-reinforcing risk-premium → debt → risk-premium loop.

    • Naive — the 10y government yield up ~+200 bp peak, persistent (a level shift, only partly decaying — GLB_USLR/YIELD10_EA/CORP_CN), rest free. The model reads a higher safe rate as tighter monetary conditions — but unlike a flight-to-quality recession (where the safe 10y falls), this looks like an unexplained tightening.
    • Restricted — keep the sovereign-yield pin and add the fiscal-distress co-movement that identifies the shock: the corporate spread up (US GLB_USBAA, Moody's Baa — toward the Fed-2024 +410 bp tail; EA EACORP), equity down ~−35–55 % (GLB_USEQ), and real GDP down (a deeper, −8.5 %-style contraction sustained 6+ quarters). The identifying restriction is that the safe sovereign yield rises WHILE equity and output collapse — the sovereign is no longer a haven (safety-premium erosion). Reads through to the fiscal block, worsening debt/GDP.

    Country notes. gov10y = GLB_USLR (US) / YIELD10_EA (EA) / CORP_CN (CN sovereign); corp = GLB_USBAA (US Baa — the more credit-sensitive, Fed-BBB-consistent lever) / EACORP (EA); equity = GLB_USEQ. CN's CORP_CN IS the sovereign yield with no separate corporate spread, so the CN override applies the risk premium to CORP_CN, drops the corporate-spread leg, and keeps equity + output-down distress sized smaller (managed market, capital-account frictions). EA magnitudes are deeper than the US (periphery sovereign-spike / sovereign-bank loop, 2010-12 + Spain 2024 FSSA).

    What changed. The US corp lever switched FAAA (Aaa) → GLB_USBAA (Baa) — the more credit-sensitive, Fed-BBB-consistent grade; spread/GDP magnitudes raised toward the documented tail (+150 bp → +350 bp Baa peak; GDP −3 % → ~−8.5 % over 6 q); and an equity-down leg added as the falsifiable distress identifier (safe yield up while equity/output collapse = the sovereign is no longer a haven).

    Using a recipe¶

    1. Pick the bloc (US / EA / CN) in the top bar.
    2. Open Cookbook ▾ in the scenario header. The list is filtered to recipes applicable to the active bloc; expand any row to read its rationale.
    3. Press Load naive to see the model's own (usually demand-led) read, then Load + restriction to see the identified shock. Compare the two — the qualitative flip is the lesson.
    4. For the cross-country answer, press Solve full GVAR after loading (v1 writes the active bloc's pins only).
    5. Save / Load still export/import a scenario to/from Excel exactly as before; the cookbook does not change that workflow.

    Recipes #3 (fiscal loosening) and #7 (deglobalization) explicitly read through to the external / NFA satellite once that block ships, and #3 / #9 to the fiscal block; the cookbook is the demand side of those satellites' pedagogy.
